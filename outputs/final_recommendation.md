# Final Business Recommendation

## Objective

The objective of this analysis was to identify the business factors most strongly associated with monthly sales and provide evidence-based recommendations to support business decision-making.

---

## Recommendation Summary

Based on the regression analysis, the **Multiple Regression Model** is recommended as the primary decision-support model because it provides the strongest explanatory power, explaining approximately **78–81% of the variation in monthly sales (R² ≈ 0.78–0.81)**. By considering multiple business variables simultaneously, it offers the most reliable insights for understanding sales performance.

---

## Key Findings

The analysis identified several variables that have the strongest association with monthly sales.

### Footfall

Footfall emerged as the **most influential predictor** of monthly sales across all models. Stores with higher customer traffic consistently achieved higher sales, making it the most important factor for leadership to monitor and improve.

### Marketing Spend

Marketing Spend showed a **positive and statistically significant** relationship with monthly sales. While its impact was smaller than Footfall, effective marketing investment contributes to attracting customers and improving overall sales performance.

### Inventory Availability

Inventory Availability was also a **significant positive predictor** of sales. Stores with better product availability were more likely to achieve higher sales, highlighting the importance of maintaining sufficient inventory to reduce missed sales opportunities.

---

## Variables That Should Not Be Over-Interpreted

The analysis also identified variables that should not be treated as major sales drivers based on the current dataset.

* **Average Discount Percentage** showed a negative relationship with sales but was not statistically significant, so pricing decisions should not rely solely on this result.
* **Customer Rating** had a positive coefficient but was not statistically significant in the final model, suggesting no clear evidence that higher ratings alone drive sales.
* **Region (North)** did not show a statistically significant difference compared with the reference region after accounting for other business factors.

These variables may still be important from a business perspective, but the current regression analysis does not provide sufficient statistical evidence to consider them primary drivers of monthly sales.

---

## Recommended Business Actions

Based on the findings, leadership should prioritize the following actions:

* Increase customer footfall through targeted marketing campaigns, customer engagement initiatives, and an improved in-store experience.
* Continue investing in marketing activities while regularly monitoring their return on investment (ROI).
* Maintain high inventory availability to minimize stock shortages and lost sales opportunities.
* Monitor store performance using multiple business metrics rather than relying on a single indicator.
* Analyze high-performing stores to identify successful operational practices that can be replicated across other locations.
* Update the regression model periodically as new business data becomes available to improve decision-making over time.

---

## Risks and Limitations

Although the final regression model explains a large proportion of sales variation, it does not account for every factor that may influence monthly sales.

Potential factors not included in the analysis include:

* Seasonal demand
* Local promotional campaigns
* Economic conditions
* Competitor activities
* Customer demographics
* Store management quality
* Other store-specific operational factors

Additionally, residual analysis showed that some stores performed substantially better or worse than predicted, indicating that additional business variables may influence sales.

Therefore, the model should be used as a **decision-support tool** rather than an exact forecasting system.

---

## Association Does Not Prove Causation

Regression analysis identifies **statistical associations** between business variables and monthly sales, but it does **not** prove that one variable directly causes another.

For example:

* Higher footfall is associated with higher sales, but increasing footfall alone does not guarantee increased sales.
* Higher marketing spend is associated with improved sales performance, but promotions, customer demand, and market conditions may also contribute to these outcomes.

Business decisions should therefore combine regression insights with operational knowledge, business context, and managerial judgment.

---

## Final Conclusion

The **Multiple Regression Model** provides the most reliable and actionable insights among the models evaluated. The analysis indicates that **Footfall, Marketing Spend, and Inventory Availability** are the strongest business drivers associated with monthly sales.

To improve sales performance, leadership should focus on:

* Increasing customer footfall
* Optimizing marketing investments
* Maintaining high inventory availability
* Monitoring performance using multiple business indicators

These findings provide a strong evidence base for strategic planning while recognizing that regression analysis supports decision-making through observed associations rather than proving direct cause-and-effect relationships.
